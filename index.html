Chapter One

What Is Deforestation?

The permanent clearing of forests for farmland, logging, settlement, and industry.

Deforestation is the large-scale removal of trees from forest land that is then converted to non-forest use. It is not the same as seasonal tree loss or sustainable harvesting: once a forest is cleared, the dense web of life it supported, the soil it held together, and the water it regulated do not simply return.

In the Philippines, forest cover has fallen from around 70 percent of the country's land area a century ago to roughly 24 percent today. Mindanao, our island, has borne a heavy share of that loss. What was once a continuous green canopy across the Davao region is now a patchwork — fragmented by plantations, mines, roads, and expanding cities.

Understanding deforestation begins with a simple idea: a forest is not just a stand of trees. It is a living system that our rivers, our climate, and our neighbours depend on.

Chapter Two

Its Effects on Davao

The loss of our forests is not an abstract statistic. It is flooding, hotter days, and rivers that no longer run clean.

Davao sits in the shadow of Mount Apo and is cradled by watersheds that feed our homes and farms. When forests on those slopes are cleared, the rain that once soaked slowly into the ground now rushes downhill. Our city has seen this cost directly — in flash floods along the Davao and Talomo rivers, in landslides in the uplands, and in sediment that muddies the coast where we fish.

The damage is felt beyond weather events. Indigenous communities, particularly the Lumad, lose the ancestral landscapes that have sustained their food, medicine, and identity for generations. Farmers lose the predictable rains that healthy forests help produce. Families lose the cooler afternoons we used to take for granted.

Flooding & Landslides

Without root systems to anchor the soil, heavy rains turn into destructive runoff across Davao's watersheds.

Hotter Days

Forests cool the air and hold moisture. Their loss pushes local temperatures higher, year after year.

Lost Biodiversity

Mindanao is home to the Philippine eagle and thousands of endemic species. Forest clearing leaves them nowhere to go.

Displaced Communities

Lumad and upland farming families depend on the forest for food, water, and cultural life.

Chapter Three

How We Can Fix It

Recovery is possible — but only when local action, policy, and everyday choices move together.

The forests we have lost will not return on their own. But Davao is not without hope. Reforestation programs along the Davao watershed have already begun to restore degraded slopes. Indigenous-led conservation in the hinterlands has protected some of the most intact forest left in Mindanao. These efforts prove that when communities lead, the land can heal.

As students of Stockbridge, our part is to learn, to speak honestly about what is happening, and to support the people already doing this work. That looks like joining tree-planting drives, choosing products not tied to illegal logging, holding local leaders accountable, and passing what we learn on to others.

Protect What Remains

Support the enforcement of existing forest protection laws and the rights of indigenous stewards.

Reforest & Restore

Join reforestation programs along the Davao, Talomo, and Lipadas watersheds.

Consume Thoughtfully

Refuse products tied to illegal logging. Choose local, ask questions, and look at supply chains.

Speak & Teach

Share what you know. Bring classmates, family, and neighbours into the conversation.
